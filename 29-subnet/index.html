29 subnet – Free IP Lookup & Guide

🗓️ Published on October 27, 2025

The 29 subnet, often referred to as a /29 subnet, plays a crucial role in IP networking by efficiently managing a limited number of IP addresses within a network segment. Understanding this subnet can enhance your networking skills and improve your ability to configure networks effectively. In this guide, we will explore the intricacies of the 29 subnet, how it works, and the differences between IPv4 and IPv6.

Overview

A /29 subnet allows for the allocation of eight IP addresses, with six usable addresses for hosts. This is particularly useful for small networks or point-to-point connections, where only a few devices need IP addresses. The remaining addresses are reserved for the subnet itself and the broadcast address.

How It Works

🔍 Check Your IP Address

In a /29 subnet, the first address is the network address, the last is the broadcast address, and the addresses in between are assigned to devices on the network. For example, in the subnet 192.168.1.0/29, the usable IP addresses would be 192.168.1.1 to 192.168.1.6, while 192.168.1.0 is the system address and 192.168.1.7 is the broadcast address.

IPv4 vs IPv6

IPv4 addresses, like those utilized in a /29 subnet, are still widely used despite the growth of IPv6. IPv6 was developed to address the limitations of IPv4, including address exhaustion. While a /29 subnet can accommodate small networks, IPv6 offers a vastly larger address space, allowing for a more extensive range of devices and improved routing efficiency. Transitioning to IPv6 can enhance the scalability of networks, which is essential for future growth.
